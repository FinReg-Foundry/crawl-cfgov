About the “Understanding Your Financial Aid Offer” tool

This page describes how our “Understanding Your Financial Aid Offer” tool works. It includes:

  • Where the data comes from
  • Explanations of the calculations
  • Definitions of financial aid terms
  • Links to resources to learn more about related topics

Using the tool

If the school has adopted the “Understanding Your Financial Aid Offer” tool, they will send you a link to the tool. Click on the link and verify your financial aid information, which is prepopulated in the tool. Then complete the additional steps of the tool before moving forward with the enrollment process.

The amounts in Step 1 have been prepopulated based on your specific financial aid information, which has been provided by the school. Your financial aid information should accurately reflect your aid offer from the school. If the information is incorrect please reach out to the school’s financial aid office and have them generate a new link to our tool.

When you use this tool, you can change those financial aid amounts to see how reducing loan amounts or increasing personal contributions can affect your overall debt. Please note that editing information in this tool will not change your financial aid offer. If you would like to discuss changes to your financial aid offer, you must talk to the school’s financial aid office.

Assumptions for calculations

The amounts in Step 1 are used to calculate an estimate of what your monthly payments will be and what you’ll pay in total over the life of your student loan. In order to create these estimates, the tool makes the following assumptions, which may not fully apply to all students:

  • Total cost of attendance, including tuition and fees, remains the same each year
  • Total amount of scholarships and grants remains the same each year
  • Amount you can contribute remains the same each year
  • Loan amounts and interest rates remain the same each year (since changes in interest rates are difficult to predict, we assume they will stay the same while you are in school)
  • Eligibility for your federal, private, and school-provided loans remain the same, and if your parents are borrowing a PLUS loan their eligibility remains the same.
  • You will successfully complete your program on time (based on the length of time you selected at the beginning of the tool)
  • You will repay your loan under the standard, 10-year repayment plan, unless you appear to meet the eligibility rules for the 25-year extended repayment plan and you select the longer repayment option.
    • The 25-year extended repayment plan is available for borrowers who owe at least $30,000 in Direct student loans at the time the borrowers enroll in the extended repayment plan.
  • You will begin repayment after you graduate, and the allotted grace period expires (between 6-9 months after graduating, depending on the type of loan)
  • For the purposes of calculating interest and repayments, we assume student loans are fully disbursed (meaning the funds are sent to the school) at the beginning of each academic year although typically they are disbursed over the course of different academic terms.
  • Eligibility requirements or types of federal aid available to you do not change while you are attending school

In reality, any of these amounts may go up or down, which would change the total amount of debt you owe and your projected monthly student loan payment.

Step 1 Reviewing your first-year offer

Cost of attendance

The total cost of attendance is the estimated amount you will be responsible for paying to attend this school for one year. It adds up all the known costs for attending the school, which include tuition and fees and allowances for books, supplies, and transportation. The total cost of attendance also includes costs that you would have regardless of attending school, such as housing and meals. The category for “other education costs” includes miscellaneous and personal expenses, such as dependent care, costs for a computer, costs related to a disability, entertainment, etc.

Scholarships and grants

This section of the tool totals up the amount you are being offered from scholarships and grants. Most scholarships and grants do not have to be repaid. However, if you withdraw from school before finishing your classes, you might have to repay part of a grant. Grants are often based on financial need, while scholarships are usually based on merit or some other personal characteristic or status.

Federal Pell Grant. This does not have to be repaid (unless, for example, you withdraw from school and owe a refund).

  • Mainly for undergraduates who have not previously earned a degree
  • Awarded based on financial need
  • Up to $X per academic year

Military tuition assistance. This does not have to be repaid (unless, for example, you withdraw from school and owe a refund).

  • For active-duty or reserve servicemembers who are pursuing higher education
  • Up to $X per academic year depending on your service

GI Bill® Benefits. This does not have to be repaid (unless, for example, you withdraw from school and owe a refund).

  • For servicemembers and veterans
  • Benefits include funds to cover tuition and fees, a monthly living allowance, and an annual book stipend

Personal and family/other contributions

This section of the tool totals the amount you, your family, or others can contribute toward the cost of school by tapping sources of funds that do not include federal or private loans that are issued in your name. Thus, your personal debt won’t increase from these types of contributions. However, this section of the tool may include contributions from others who took out loans they will need to repay. For example, in this section, you should enter the amount of a Parent PLUS loan that a parent is taking out on your behalf, but do not enter the amount of a private loan issued to you and co-signed by a parent or anyone else.

Money you earn from a job while enrolled in school should be entered in “Cash you will pay,” unless it is earned from an approved Federal Work-Study program.

Parents of undergraduate students may be eligible to receive a Parent PLUS loan, which is very similar to a Grad PLUS loan. If your parents take out a Parent PLUS loan to help pay for college costs, enter it in this section. Since these loans are ones that someone else repays, they are not included in any summaries of your debt within the tool. This tool only measures your debt, not your family’s debt.

Federal student loans

This section of the tool totals the amount of federal loans you are being offered. All calculations in Step 1 for federal loans assume a 10-year repayment period that starts after you leave school. However, interest may start accruing before the 10-year repayment period begins.

For federal student loans, the tool applies the interest rates that apply to loans first disbursed during the 2016-17 financial aid award year. These rates are set by the laws governing federal student loan programs, including the Higher Education Act.

The total amount of federal aid you receive (including grants, scholarships, and loans) cannot be more than the total cost of attendance at the school (which includes tuition, fees, books, and supplies). Generally you must be enrolled in school at least half-time to qualify for federal loans.

Types of federal student loans

Perkins loan. This is a subsidized federal loan available at some schools. If the school does not participate in the Perkins loan program, you will not see this as an option under federal loans.

  • Awarded based on financial need
  • Up to $X per academic year for undergraduates
  • Up to $X for graduate students
  • [XX]% interest rate fixed for the life of the loan
  • Repayment starts nine months after you leave school or drop below half time (grace period)
  • Interest doesn’t start accruing until you leave school and your grace period ends
  • No fee to take out loan
  • Due to limited funds for this type of loan, not everyone who qualifies for this program will receive one

Subsidized loan. This is a federal loan also known as a Direct Subsidized Loan.

  • Awarded based on financial need to undergraduate students
  • Up to $X per academic year for the first year
  • The maximum amount you can borrow per year increases every year you are in school
  • [XX]% interest rate fixed for the life of the loan
  • Repayment starts six months after you leave school (grace period)
  • The Department of Education pays any interest accruing on your loans while you are in school (attending at least half-time) and during your grace period
  • Loan fee of [XX]% of the total loan amount
  • Graduate students are not eligible

Unsubsidized loan. This is a federal loan also known as a Direct Unsubsidized Loan.

  • For all students, regardless of financial need
  • Up to $X for the first year in school for dependent undergraduates
    • Increases as you progress through school, up to $X
  • Up to $X for the first year for independent undergraduate students (students who are not required to provide parental information when applying for federal financial aid)
    • Increases as you progress through school, up to $X
  • Up to $X for graduate or professional students – higher annual limits apply to students pursuing degrees in the health professions.
  • If you take out both subsidized and unsubsidized loans the subsidized loans count toward the unsubsidized loan cap. For example, if you are a dependent student and received $3,500 in subsidized loans you would only be eligible for $2,000 in unsubsidized loans your first year.
  • [XX]% interest rate for undergraduates fixed for the life of the loan
  • [XX]% interest rate for graduate or professional students fixed for the life of the loan
  • Interest starts accruing while you are still in school
  • Repayment starts six months after you leave school (grace period)
  • Loan fee of [XX]% of the total loan amount

Grad PLUS loan. This is a federal loan also known as a Direct PLUS Loan. If you are not enrolling in a graduate program you will not see this loan option.

  • For all graduate and professional students
  • Available to those who need to borrow more than the maximum unsubsidized loan amounts to cover the cost of attendance
  • Eligibility is based on the borrower’s credit history, but the credit check does not look at the borrower’s credit score or income
  • Can borrow up to the program’s cost of attendance, minus any other financial assistance received , including other federal loans
  • [XX]% interest rate fixed for the life of the loan
  • Interest starts accruing while you are still in school
  • Repayment starts six months after you leave school (deferment period)
  • Loan fee of [XX]% of the total loan amount

Private student loans and payment plans

This section of the tool totals the amount of private loans you are being offered, including tuition payment plans offered by some schools. All calculations in Step 1 for private loans assume a 10-year repayment period that starts after you leave school. However, interest may start accruing before the 10-year repayment period begins.

Private loans. Private loans are offered by some banks, credit unions, schools, and state agencies. This tool allows you to input the interest rate to match what you have been offered in your financial aid package or add multiple private loans, if necessary. The specific terms of each private loan can vary, and private loans are not always available.

  • For undergraduate or graduate students
  • Requires credit check
  • Borrowers may need to apply with cosigner to meet credit standards or qualify for a lower interest rate
  • Usually carry higher interest rates than federal loans; many private loan products charge variable rates that change over the life of the loan, unlike federal loans
  • Interest generally starts accruing while you are still in school
  • Repayment typically starts after you leave school, and most lenders currently offer a six-month grace period. Some private loans programs require borrowers to start making monthly payments during school or right after graduation.
  • Usually do not offer the flexible repayment options that are available for federal student loans

You should explore all grant, scholarship and federal loan options before taking out a private student loan. If you think you will need a private student loan, you have the right to shop around to find the best deal.

Tuition payment plans. This is a financial arrangement made directly with the school. The school may also refer to this as a “retail installment contract.”

  • For undergraduate or graduate students
  • May require credit check and/or a cosigner to qualify
  • Usually carry higher interest rates than federal loans; many private loan products charge variable rates that change over the life of the loan, unlike federal loans
  • Interest doesn’t start accruing until you leave school
  • Repayment likely starts while you are still in school
  • Usually do not offer the flexible repayment options available for federal student loans

For private student loans where an interest rate is not provided we assume a constant interest rate of [XX]%. The tool allows you to adjust that interest rate to match what you have been offered by your lender. However, keep in mind that interest rates on private student loans and or tuition payment plans may be variable and change significantly throughout the life of the loan.

Calculations for the “What does this mean for your future?” section

Based on what you’ve entered in Step 1, we’ve calculated an estimated total cost of your loans after all the interest and fees are taken into account. To do this, we take a single year’s worth of loans and multiply that by how many years you’ve indicated you’ll attend the program to earn a degree. This gives you the “total cost of repayment with interest and fees.” If it takes you longer to complete the program or get the degree, your cost could be higher.

Remember, these calculations are estimates based on the assumptions that program cost, financial aid eligibility, and your annual financial contribution will remain the same for every year you attend the school. Your individual circumstances may be different.

Step 2 Weighing the financial impact of your offer

Graduation rate

The graduation rate shown is for first-time, full-time undergraduate students who earned a certificate or degree in your program and attended the school for the entire length of the program (it doesn’t include transfer or part-time students).

If the school primarily awards bachelor’s degrees, the graduation rate shown is for students who enrolled at the school six years ago. The graduation rate counts those who graduated within 150 percent of the program’s length. Thus, the graduation rate for a four-year bachelor’s degree program includes students who completed their degrees within six years of their initial enrollment. If the school primarily awards certificates or associate’s degrees, the graduation rate shown is for students who entered the school three years ago.

If the school did not provide graduation rates for your program of study, because they do not have sufficient data in order to calculate a graduation rate or were not required to calculate one, the tool will show the graduation rate for the entire school. This is based on first-time, full-time undergraduate students You can see other school-level data via the Department of Education’s College Scorecard.

The graduation rate for a school is calculated from data the school submits to the U.S. Department of Education’s Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System (IPEDS). This may not represent all undergraduates who attend this school, because it excludes part-time students and students who were previously enrolled at another post-secondary institution.

Taking on too much debt

The school provides the median salary for students graduating from your program. These salaries are calculated using the particular methods outlined by the Department of Education in its Gainful Employment rules (34 C.F.R. 668.413(b)(8)). Please remember, these salaries are based on students who started your program several years ago. Economic conditions regularly change, and your experience may be different.

Some loans, like subsidized federal loans, don’t grow larger with interest until it is time to start making payments (usually six to nine months after you leave school or if you drop below half time, depending on the type of loan).

Other loans, like unsubsidized federal loans, start accruing interest after they’ve been disbursed (paid to the school for tuition). So even though you aren’t making payments on these loans while in school, the interest is starting to accrue during that time.

The “debt when repayment begins” includes your total loan amounts for the length of the program, plus any interest and fees that have accrued while you are in school.

The general rule of thumb for undergraduates is that your “debt when repayment begins” should not exceed your anticipated annual salary the first year out of school. This guideline, which is cited by some financial aid commentators, is meant to serve as a guidepost to help you determine if you may be borrowing too much.

However, like any rule of thumb, it is a broad generalization that doesn’t apply to every person or every situation. You should consider all your options before making a decision.

If you are considering a career in public service, you should explore the U.S. Department of Education’s income-driven repayment plans and Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) program, which could result in lower monthly payments and eventual forgiveness of some federal student loan debt. Whatever the case, the rule of thumb is meant to assist with your decision-making process, not to dictate it.

Monthly student loan payment

To determine your estimated monthly student loan payment, we take your “total at repayment” from Step 1 and calculate your payments (accounting for accrued interest) over a standard 10-year repayment period, which would be 120 months.

If you owe more than $30,000 in Federal Direct loans, you are eligible to enroll in the extended repayment plan, which can stretch the term of the loan from 10 years to 25 years. Depending on the estimated amount of your Federal Direct loans, the tool will give you the option to “toggle” between a 10-year and 25-year repayment period. This feature will illustrate how lengthening the term will lower the monthly payment but increase total repayment costs.

One thing to consider before choosing a longer repayment period is that the longer the repayment period is, the more you pay in interest over the total life of the loan.

Also, federal student loans have other repayment plan options for repaying your federal student loans, including Pay as You Earn (PAYE) and Income-Based Repayment Plans (IBR), which are based on your income. Your loan servicer will notify you of your repayment options before your grace period ends. Also, you have the right to switch to another available repayment plan at any time during the repayment of your loans. Learn about other repayment options.

Affording your student loan payment

The school provides an average percentage of students who graduate from your program and get a job in their field of study within six months of graduation (not including a number of exceptions). This is known as a job placement rate. It is important to understand that there is no standard formula for calculating a job placement rate, which means that schools calculate the job placement rate differently using different assumptions. You should ask the school for details on how its job placement rate is calculated in order to understand the assumptions underlying the job placement rate calculation.

Budget worksheet. This section shows large categories of typical expenses once you leave school. They are prepopulated with data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics’ Consumer Expenditure (BLS CE) Survey , based on the average expenses for someone earning your projected yearly salary and living in one of four broadly defined geographical regions in the United States (the Midwest, the Northeast, the South, and the West). The tool defaults to the region where the school is located, but you can change that if you plan to live somewhere else after graduation.

Categories of BLS CE data:

  • Housing (includes utilities, cell phone bills, and house repairs)
  • Food
  • Clothing (apparel and services)
  • Transportation
  • Healthcare (includes health insurance and prescriptions)
  • Entertainment spending
  • Pension and Social Security
  • Taxes
  • Other (cash contributions, alcohol, tobacco, personal care products and services, reading, education, and miscellaneous expenses)

You can also change any of the default average amounts shown in the worksheet to better represent your actual budget once you leave school.

The tool then takes your average monthly salary (yearly salary divided by 12) and subtracts your estimated monthly student loan payment and other expenses indicated to estimate how much is left over at the end of the month. This may be a negative number, showing that your costs are more than your projected income.

Keep in mind that the expenses in the tool are for an average household, rather than an individual student. Your own expenses may differ depending on your age, family size, and other factors. For example, the cost of living in your particular town or city may differ substantially from the average cost of living in the relevant geographic region.

Debt burden. This section shows you how much of your projected monthly salary you’ll be spending on student loan payments, also referred to as your monthly debt burden. The higher your student loan payment, the higher your debt burden.

If you owe more than $30,000 in Federal Direct loans, you are eligible for the extended repayment plan, which can stretch the term of the loan from 10 years to 25 years. If your anticipated, post-school Direct loan balance exceeds $30,000, the tool will give you an option to “toggle” between a 10-year and 25-year repayment period, which will show your debt burden under either the 10-year or 25-year payback period. The extended repayment plan will reduce your monthly payment and reduce your monthly debt burden but it will also significantly increase your total interest expenses.

There are also alternative repayment plans based on income and family size that we do not feature on our tool. These repayment plans have lower student loan payments when you have a lower salary. For instance, if you have a family of four with an annual income of $50,000, your monthly student loan payment would be $114. It could be as low as $0, depending on your income and family size. If you select one of these income- based repayment plans you may be paying off your loans for up to 20 to 25 years.

These repayment plans typically aren’t available for private student loans. If you’re still not able to make payments on your loans, you could go into default.

Loan default rate

The loan default rate is the percentage of a school’s borrowers who entered the repayment period on their federal loans during the period beginning three years ago on October 1 and who defaulted before September 30 of last year. Specifically, “loan default rate” refers to the program’s three-year cohort default rate as calculated according to 34 CFR 668.413(b)(13).

When a borrower fails to make scheduled payments on student loans over a certain period of time, his or her loans will be called into “default”. The loan default rate shown in the tool is for students in the program who started having to repay their student loans during the period beginning three years ago on October 1, but have gone into default sometime prior to September 30 of the past year. For instance, if a student graduated five years ago, has been making payments on the loan, and then goes into default, that default is not counted in the three-year default rate.

If the school did not provide a default rate for your program of study, the tool will show the default rate for the entire school. Data used to calculate the school’s default rate is drawn from the Department of Education’s National Student Loan Data System (NSLDS) .

You can see other school-level data at the Department of Education’s College Navigator .

Step 3 Consider your options

After you answer the question between Steps 2 and Steps 3, we send a notification to the school telling them you’ve completed the tool. Once the school has been notified, you are able to move forward in the enrollment process.

Keep in mind that we only notify the school when you complete the tool; we do not share with them any of your responses to our questions or edits you made to your aid offer.

Remember that any changes you made to your offer in Step 1 do not affect your actual financial aid offer or your ability to accept the original offer.

You will need to contact the school’s financial aid representative and work with them to have your financial aid package updated if you’d like to make changes to your original financial aid offer.

Lastly, make sure you have promptly provided any documents the school has requested, as the school may need to receive those documents before you will be able to receive any type of grant or loans.

In Step 3, we provide some ideas for either decreasing the amount that you borrow or increasing your post-graduation earnings. This list only contains a few ideas for ways that you might better afford attending school. More guidance on preparing for college is available at the Department of Education .
